Project summary

Integrative Single-Cell Transcriptomics in Thyroid Cancer Dedifferentiation

This project studies how normal thyroid epithelial states transition toward papillary thyroid carcinoma and anaplastic thyroid carcinoma using single-cell transcriptomics, trajectory inference, gene regulatory network analysis, and CNV-aware interpretation.

Back to homepage Download CV

Status

Manuscript in preparation

Computational candidates under experimental validation.

Biological question

What changes as thyroid epithelial states move through disease progression?

01 The project asks which cell states, gene expression programs, and candidate regulators are associated with the Normal to PTC to ATC transition.

02 The goal is to use computational evidence to reason about transitional cellular states and regulatory programs without treating any single method as the endpoint.

My role

Lead computational and bioinformatics analysis

01 Lead computational and bioinformatics analysis I lead the computational and bioinformatics analysis, including preprocessing, annotation, trajectory inference, dynamic gene expression analysis, gene regulatory network inference, CNV-aware interpretation, and candidate regulator prioritization.

02 Integrating evidence into biologically meaningful hypotheses Connecting outputs into a coherent biological interpretation is a central challenge. A major challenge in this project is not only running individual analyses, but connecting their outputs into a coherent biological interpretation. I integrate evidence from trajectory structure, dynamic gene expression, regulatory network inference, and CNV-aware tumor analysis to distinguish robust biological signals from method-specific noise. This step is essential for prioritizing candidate genes and regulatory programs that are not just statistically interesting, but biologically plausible and suitable for experimental follow-up.

Collaboration

Working closely with biological collaborators

This project has involved continuous collaboration with biologists and clinical collaborators. Frequent research discussions, including daily meetings during active analysis phases, helped keep computational decisions closely tied to the biological question rather than treating the pipeline as a purely technical workflow.

These discussions helped me interpret single-cell results, distinguish technical artifacts from biologically meaningful patterns, refine analysis choices using experimental context, and prioritize candidate genes and regulatory programs for experimental follow-up. This shaped how I approach computational biology: as an iterative dialogue between biological insight, statistical reasoning, and reproducible computational work.
